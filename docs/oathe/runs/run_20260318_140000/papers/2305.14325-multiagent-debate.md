# Improving Factuality and Reasoning in Language Models through Multiagent Debate

**arxiv**: 2305.14325
**Perspective**: MULTI_AGENT_DEBATE

## Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in language generation, understanding, and few-shot learning. This paper presents a complementary approach to improve language responses where multiple language model instances propose and debate their individual responses and reasoning processes over multiple rounds to arrive at a common final answer. Findings indicate that this approach significantly enhances mathematical and strategic reasoning across a number of tasks. The approach also improves the factual validity of generated content, reducing fallacious answers and hallucinations. The approach may be directly applied to existing black-box models and uses identical procedure and prompts for all tasks investigated. Overall, the "society of minds" approach has the potential to significantly advance the capabilities of LLMs.

## Key Mechanism

Given a question, multiple agents (copies of an LLM) generate answers. Each response serves as a possible thought process or source of information which agents may re-examine. After initial responses, a round of debate begins: individual responses from other agents are concatenated and given as context to each agent, with each agent instructed to construct a new response. Each agent is responsible for both verifying responses given by other agents AND refining its own response. This iterates over multiple rounds.

## Critical Findings

1. **Convergence**: Agents empirically converge on a single shared answer after multiple rounds of debate
2. **Stubbornness matters**: Prompts that encouraged models to be more "stubborn" (trust their own solutions more) led to LONGER debates and BETTER final solutions
3. **Agreeableness problem**: Language model agents are relatively "agreeable", perhaps as a result of instruction tuning or RLHF — they converge TOO QUICKLY
4. **Debate duration control**: Can be controlled by varying how much a model trusts its own outputs over those generated by other models
5. **Orthogonal to existing prompting**: Works alongside chain-of-thought, self-consistency, etc.
